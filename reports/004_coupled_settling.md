# MVP 0.4 Coupled Settling

Generated by:

```bash
PYTHONPATH=src:. python3 experiments/coupled_settling.py
```

## Purpose

MVP 0.3 showed that a one-pass joint content+temporal score rescues the tight
content-family failure. MVP 0.4 turns that score into an iterative settling
process and records the trajectory.

The loop is:

1. Score candidate anchors by content compatibility plus temporal compatibility.
2. Use the anchor distribution to read a temporal state.
3. Blend the original temporal cue with the read temporal state.
4. Repeat until weights stabilize or `max_iter` is reached.

## Strong Temporal Cue

Configuration:

- `family_noise = 0.10`
- `content_beta = 100.0`
- `temporal_beta = 100.0`
- `feedback = 0.75`

Result:

| Method | Temporal-neighbor Recall@4 |
|---|---:|
| One-pass joint read | 1.000 |
| Coupled settling | 1.000 |

Trajectory:

| Iteration | Top anchor | Top weight | Entropy |
|---:|---|---:|---:|
| 1 | `stair` | 1.000 | 0.000 |
| 2 | `stair` | 1.000 | 0.000 |

Interpretation: when temporal evidence is strong, the coupled loop commits
immediately and stabilizes. Iteration does not improve recall because one-pass
already solves the case.

Interpretation: with weak temporal pressure, the system keeps the correct anchor
on top but remains partially contested. This is useful: the trajectory exposes
uncertainty even when top-k recall succeeds. That gives us the first concrete
version of "engagement/resolution" as measurable dynamics.

## Architectural Takeaway

The coupled loop matters less as an accuracy booster in this toy case and more
as a diagnostic surface. It gives us the path, not just the endpoint: immediate
commitment under strong evidence, stable ambiguity under weak evidence.

The next step is a cue-degradation sweep: vary temporal cue quality and measure
when the loop commits, stays ambiguous, or flips to a distractor.
